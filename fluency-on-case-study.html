01 / The Problem

Google Drive, WhatsApp, and a spreadsheet. That was the school.

Before Fluency On existed, Jamile ran her entire school manually. Lessons and homework lived in shared Google Drive folders. All communication with students happened over WhatsApp. Progress was tracked on a spreadsheet she updated by hand after every session.

It worked, but only because she compensated for it personally. Every hour she spent managing files and chasing messages was an hour she wasn’t teaching.

“I feel like I run a small tech company just to teach English.”Jamile / Founder & Teacher
How it actually worked
01
Lectures and homework uploaded to Google Drive folders, shared individually per student
02
All communication through WhatsApp: scheduling, questions, feedback, reminders
03
Progress tracking on a spreadsheet she updated herself after each session
04
At-risk detection happened when a student messaged her first. There was no other signal.
02 / Research

Four interviews. One system held together by habit.

I interviewed Jamile and three students at different proficiency levels. I asked each of them to walk me through a real session: how they found their materials, how they communicated, how they knew what to do next. The Google Drive and WhatsApp setup came up immediately. Students had adapted to it. Jamile had built workarounds on top of workarounds.

↳ Key Insight

Two separate information architectures were needed, not one with role-filtered content. The teacher’s job is oversight of people. The student’s job is knowing what to do next. Merging them created invisible friction for both.

03 / Strategy

Two mental models. One coherent system.

The goal wasn’t to digitise the Google Drive and WhatsApp setup. It was to replace it entirely. The system needed to do what Jamile was doing manually: deliver content, track progress, surface problems, and keep students oriented, without any of it touching her calendar.

Each portal was designed around one question. Everything that didn’t answer that question was friction the platform should absorb.

Teacher Portal
Who needs my attention?
  • Students & progress
  • Course assignment
  • At-risk detection
  • Quick onboarding
Student Portal
What do I do today?
  • Continue where I left off
  • One clear next step
  • Visible progress
  • Streak & consistency

06 / Design Iterations + Analytics

Three versions before both sides felt obvious.

V1Single unified dashboard

One interface, content filtered by role.

Problem: Cognitive overload for both. Each side constantly saw the wrong structure.

V2Role dashboards, shared layout

Different widgets, same navigation system.

Problem: Teachers and students navigate differently. The shared sidebar confused both.

V3Separate IA, shared design system

Completely separate portals built on the same visual language.

Result: Both sides felt obvious from the first click.

07 / Engineering Shaped by Research

What users said changed how I built the backend.

Research finding

Jamile didn’t know students were falling behind until they messaged her. Detection had to be automatic and immediate.

Engineering response

Real-time progress persistence layer recalculates at-risk thresholds on every lesson interaction. No batch jobs, no manual checks.

Research finding

Role confusion in V1/V2; UI-layer filtering alone wasn’t enough. Teachers sometimes accessed student data.

Engineering response

RBAC at the database layer. Queries scoped by role before data reaches the client. Separation is structural.

Research finding

Vanessa reopened the same lesson twice. Completion state had to be unambiguous and durable across sessions.

Engineering response

Explicit completion state stored per-lesson, per-user at DB level. “Continue where you left off” is computed, never inferred from timestamps.

09 / What I Learned
01

The messiest systems are the hardest to question.

Jamile had been running on Drive and WhatsApp long enough that it felt normal. It took mapping it step by step, together, for the cost to become visible.

02

Role-based UX starts at architecture, not UI.

Content filtering doesn’t fix misaligned IA. V1 and V2 proved it. V3 fixed it at the foundation.

03

Momentum is a product feature.

Mariana didn’t wanted to feel improvement. Streak, history, and skill labels were deliberate design decisions.

04

The most powerful decisions are eliminations.

If a feature didn’t answer “who needs attention?” or “what do I do today?” it was cut.

Teachers should teach. Students should learn. The system should handle the rest.
